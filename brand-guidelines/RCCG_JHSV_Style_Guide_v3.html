Visual Style Guide · v1.0

A church website that feels like walking in, not logging in.

This guide defines the visual system for RCCG Jesus House Silicon Valley. It translates who we are — a multicultural, dynamic family of faith in the Bay Area — into a design language that feels bold, warm, distinct, and deeply welcoming. Built to feel modern without feeling corporate. Reverent without feeling dated.

April 2026 · Based on the JHSV Design Review & Sitemap Blueprint
01

Design Principles

Before we talk color or type, we name what we are — and what we refuse to be. These six principles are the lens every design decision is checked against.

Warmth beats polish.

A SaaS site optimizes for conversion; a church site optimizes for welcome. If a choice makes the page feel more "human hands made this," we keep it. If it makes it feel more "a growth team A/B tested this," we cut it.

Bold, not loud.

Confident typography, a strong accent color, and one distinctive motif (the arch) give the site presence. Noise, gradients-on-everything, and glowing buttons do not. We earn attention through quality, not decibel.

One voice, not six.

The current site runs six font families. We run two — a warm display serif and a friendly sans. This single change does more for perceived quality than any other decision in this document.

Every element earns its place.

Jony Ive's rule, adapted. If a decorative button, a text shadow, or a tiled pattern doesn't serve the visitor's next step, it comes out. White space is not empty — it's rest.

Designed for a Sunday morning phone.

The first-time visitor is pulling into the parking lot, checking the address. Buttons are thumb-sized. Type is readable at arm's length. Load is fast on 4G. Nothing important hides below a fold.

The arch is the soul.

One repeating shape runs through the site — the rounded archway. It tops photographs, frames section dividers, and holds icons. It's the "front door" motif, used with restraint so it signals us, not overwhelms.

03

Typography

Two fonts. One voice. A warm display serif for moments that should feel like they're speaking to you, paired with a friendly sans for everything you need to read, click, or navigate.

Display — DM Serif Display

The Display Face · For H1, H2, and pull quotes only (32px and up)
Come as you are.
DM SERIF DISPLAY · REGULAR · ITALIC

The quick brown fox jumps over the lazy dog — and finds himself welcomed home on a Sunday morning.

DM Serif Display is a high-contrast display serif — thick verticals, hairline horizontals — built for moments that should feel confident and present. At 56px and up, those contrasted strokes look sharp and editorial. The italic carries dramatic energy, ideal for the kind of emphasis we want in hero headlines ("come as you are"). It ships in just one weight (Regular) plus its italic, which forces the discipline we want: this face is only ever used at display sizes — 32px and above. Anything smaller falls to Manrope. Stretching DM Serif Display to small sizes makes the thin strokes fragile and the page feels weak; respecting its scale makes the page feel intentional.

Sans — Manrope

The UI face · For H3–H6, body, navigation, buttons, forms
Plan Your Visit
MANROPE · WEIGHTS 300 / 400 / 500 / 600 / 700

A multicultural and dynamic church where people from a variety of backgrounds and walks of life come together to worship and to connect.

Manrope is friendlier than Inter and warmer than Helvetica. Its slightly rounded terminals and generous apertures make it readable at every size without feeling corporate or toy-like. Because DM Serif Display is restricted to 32px and above, Manrope carries more of the typographic load on this site than in most two-font systems — it owns navigation, body, buttons, forms, and all subheads down to card titles. We stick to three weights: 400 Regular for body, 500 Medium for navigation and small labels, 600 SemiBold for buttons, card titles, and subheads. Never above 600 — heavier weights compete with the drama of DM Serif Display's display moments.

Typography rules

Always sentence case. Never ALL CAPS except the tiny eyebrow label. The current site uses "WELCOME! WE ARE GLAD YOU ARE HERE" — we don't do that. Sentence case feels human; caps feel shouty.

No text shadows, ever. Removed in the review; stays removed.

DM Serif Display lives at 32px and above — never below. This is the most important rule in the whole guide. The thin strokes that make this typeface beautiful at hero size become fragile at small sizes. Card titles, subheads, body, navigation — everything below 32px — uses Manrope. Respecting this boundary is what keeps the page feeling sharp; breaking it is what makes a high-contrast serif look weak.

Italics are emphasis, not decoration. DM Serif Display's italics carry real drama — use them 1–2 words at a time inside a headline ("come as you are"), not for whole paragraphs.

Line length 45–75 characters. Bodies wider than 75 characters get hard to track on big screens. Use max-width 680px on the body column, never more.

Where the arch appears

Image frames are the arch's primary habitat. Portrait photography — pastors, congregation candids, ministry leads — is always framed inside a full arch. Landscape photos (event shots, sanctuary interiors) stay as rounded rectangles.

Portrait — Pastor bio
Community — Get Connected
Editorial moment

Rules of the arch

Two arches per viewport, maximum. If a section already has an arched image, don't add an arched icon nearby. The arch earns its power through restraint.

Never arch a button. Buttons are pills or rounded rectangles. An arched button reads as a tombstone.

Arches open upward. Never flip one so the rounded side is on the bottom — it reads as a keystone, not an entryway.

Inside dark sections, arches can glow softly as atmospheric background elements (see the hero demo below). Used as light-through-stained-glass, they transform from a shape into an ambience.

Buttons

Pill-shaped, warm, confident. Never more than one filled primary button visible per viewport — clarity of the next action matters more than button variety.

On light backgrounds (primary site surfaces)
Plan Your Visit → Give Now Learn More See all sermons →
Primary (plum) is the default. Coral accent is reserved for give/action emphasis. Outline for secondary, ghost for tertiary.
On dark backgrounds (footer, hero overlay, CTA bands)
Plan Your Visit → Watch Live Explore sermons

Button rules

Minimum height 48px on every button, everywhere. Makes thumb-targeting easy on mobile; doesn't feel small on desktop.

Always pill-shaped (border-radius 999px). Pills read warmer than rounded rectangles — they match the brand's welcoming tone. Exception: form submit buttons inside dense forms can be rounded-rect to match the input shape.

Arrow glyph on link-style buttons only. Not on primary CTAs (the button is the action; an arrow is redundant). Arrows animate 4px right on hover to hint at forward motion without being loud about it.

One primary per viewport. If your hero has "Plan Your Visit" as the primary, everything else in that viewport is outline or ghost. Two filled primaries halve the attention on each.

Card anatomy

Image zone (aspect 3:2 default, or 4:5 with arch-top for portrait-oriented cards like "Get Connected"). Always real photography — never stock. A soft bottom gradient preserves legibility if text overlays later.

Eyebrow label (Coral, uppercase 12px) sets context in one or two words. Examples: "This Sunday" / "Latest Sermon" / "Get Connected."

Title (Manrope SemiBold, 22px, sentence case). Card titles sit below DM Serif Display's 32px floor, so they live in the sans family. Written as a hook, not a header. "When faith feels quiet" beats "Upcoming Sermon."

Description (Manrope, 14.5px). One to two sentences. Tells the visitor why they should click. Does not repeat the title.

Link, not a button. Cards use text links ("Watch now →"), not filled CTAs. Buttons on every card create visual noise; the whole card is already the affordance.

Lift on hover (translateY(-2px), shadow grows, border warms to Soft Plum). The gesture is tiny — it acknowledges the pointer without performing for it.

08

Forms

Forms are the site's moments of highest vulnerability — a first-time visitor sharing their name, a prayer request sent into the unknown. They must feel safe. Large touch targets, quiet labels, clear focus states, no dark patterns.

Let us know you're coming

We'll send a short note so you know what to expect on Sunday — no spam, promise.

Let us know you're coming

Form rules

Input height 48px minimum. Padded 14px vertical, 16px horizontal. Never a cramped input.

Labels sit above fields, never inside. In-field labels that animate away (Material Design style) confuse older users and are a pain on mobile autofill.

Focus states are loud on purpose. 4px plum-tinted halo + white background + plum border. On a warm parchment page, the focused field should feel like a spotlight.

The submit button says what happens. "Let us know you're coming" — not "Submit." Every form button echoes the form's purpose in the visitor's own words.

No required-field asterisks. Inline text ("optional") is gentler and more modern. Assume fields are required unless marked otherwise.

09

Motion & Micro-interaction

Restraint. Animation exists to acknowledge the visitor's action and soften transitions — never to impress. If you notice the motion, we've overdone it.

Default ease
cubic-bezier(0.22, 1, 0.36, 1)

Our out-curve. Fast entry, gentle settle. Feels natural, not mechanical. Used on 95% of transitions.

Button hover
250ms

Background shifts 1 shade darker, lifts 1px, soft shadow blooms. Arrow glyph slides 4px right.

Card hover
300ms

translateY(-2px), shadow grows softer & larger, border warms to Soft Plum. A physical acknowledgment, not a dance.

Hero entrance
600ms + 80ms stagger

Eyebrow → headline → lede → CTA. Each element fades up 12px. Plays once on load.

Scroll reveal
500ms

Section content fades + lifts 16px as it enters viewport. IntersectionObserver with prefers-reduced-motion bypass.

Focus ring
150ms

Form field halo appears instantly but softly — the visitor should feel the field "receive" their attention.

Accessibility

Every animation must respect @media (prefers-reduced-motion: reduce). When reduced-motion is on, durations drop to 0.01s — elements still change state, they just don't travel.

What we do — and what we don't

A checklist for any design decision. When in doubt, run the choice against this list.

We do
  • Use two fonts: DM Serif Display (display, 32px+) and Manrope (everything else)
  • Write in sentence case, even in buttons and eyebrows
  • Frame portrait photography inside the arch shape
  • Keep 60% parchment, 25% plum, 10% coral, 5% everything else
  • Let pages breathe — 80px+ between sections on desktop
  • Use italics sparingly, 1–2 words at a time, for emphasis
  • Write warmly: "we saved you a seat" not "new visitor form"
  • Make primary CTAs pill-shaped and 48px tall
  • Keep body text under 75 characters per line
  • Test every pairing for WCAG AA contrast
We don't
  • Mix more than two type families on a page
  • Use ALL CAPS (except the tiny eyebrow label)
  • Add text shadows, stroke outlines, or glow effects
  • Use pure white (#FFFFFF) as a page background
  • Arch a button. Buttons are pills. Arches frame images.
  • Use gradients as a decorative default — only for atmospheric hero overlays
  • Write in "corporate church" voice ("Experience community. Encounter God!")
  • Stack more than 2 filled primary CTAs in one viewport
  • Use stock photography — every photo is real JHSV
  • Animate things just because we can

iPad-specific behavior

Split-screen / Slide Over

Test at 50% iPad screen width (~512px on iPad Pro). Mobile layout should activate at this width.

No hover

iPad has no hover state. Never put critical info or navigation behind :hover.

Orientation

Portrait gets the tablet layout; landscape gets the small-desktop layout.

Safe areas

20px minimum padding from edges so rounded-corner displays don't clip CTAs.

Performance budget

A Sunday-morning phone on patchy 4G in a parking lot is the device this site is designed for. Hard limits on weight and Core Web Vitals; everything else is a budget call.

Image rules

Photography is always real JHSV, never stock. Format: WebP with AVIF fallback. Hero photos: 2400 × 1600px source, compressed to < 300 KB via squoosh.app at quality ~80. Card thumbnails: 800px wide, < 80 KB. Always specify width and height attributes to lock layout and protect CLS.

Font loading

Both fonts load from Google Fonts via the existing preconnect + display=swap pattern in the document head. We load only the weights we use: DM Serif Display (Regular + Italic) and Manrope (300 / 400 / 500 / 600 / 700). No additional weights, no additional families — ever.

Google Business Profile

The single highest-impact channel for local discovery. Claim or create the listing at business.google.com, complete every field (name, category = Church, address, phone, website, hours), upload 10+ real photos, post weekly with the upcoming sermon topic, and ask members to leave reviews — target 20+ in the first month.

Branded email

The current public email (rccgjhsv2013@gmail.com) undermines credibility when the church already owns rccgjhsv.org. Migrate to info@rccgjhsv.org using domain-registrar email forwarding plus a Gmail "Send as" alias. Update the schema, the footer, and /contact in the same pass.
